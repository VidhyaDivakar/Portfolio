Give your best in whatever you do — but should we depend on others' approval to feel good about our accomplishments?

We are often taught to seek feedback, to look for validation from supervisors, mentors, or loved ones. And while that feedback has value, there is a risk in making it the measure of our worth. When praise becomes the thing we work for, the absence of it feels like failure — even when the work itself was excellent.

## The Trap of External Validation

External validation is unreliable by nature. People are busy, distracted, and operating from their own perspectives. A manager who doesn't acknowledge your effort may simply not have noticed, or may express appreciation differently than you expect. If we calibrate our sense of achievement to their responses, we hand over control of something deeply personal.

The alternative is to develop a clear internal sense of what "good work" looks like for you — and to trust that sense.

## Following Your Goals, Not Just Your Dreams

"Follow your dreams" is advice that sounds empowering but can be vague. A more grounded version: pursue your self-set goals with intention. Know what you're working toward. Track your own progress. Celebrate milestones that you define.

This doesn't mean ignoring feedback — it means processing it without letting it destabilise you. Criticism can be useful information. Compliments can be encouraging. Neither should fundamentally alter your direction unless you also believe they should.

## What Inner Fire Actually Means

The concept of inner fire is not about intensity or hustle. It's about having a genuine reason — one that belongs to you — for doing what you do. It's the quiet engine that keeps running when no one is watching.

When you work from that place, the work itself becomes the reward. Recognition, when it comes, is a bonus — not the goal.

---

Maintain focus on your aspirations. Recognise your own strength and value. Understand that sustained internal motivation is more reliable than anything someone else can offer you.

That's the fire worth keeping.
